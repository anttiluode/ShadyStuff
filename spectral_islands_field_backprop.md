## Part II: Ephaptic Coupling as Weak Backpropagation

### 2.1 Standard Backpropagation — Formal Statement

In a network with $L$ layers, weights $W^{(l)}$, activations $a^{(l)}$, and loss $\mathcal{L}$, backpropagation computes:

$$\boldsymbol{\delta}^{(l)} = \left(\mathbf{W}^{(l+1)}\right)^\top \boldsymbol{\delta}^{(l+1)} \odot f'\bigl(\mathbf{z}^{(l)}\bigr)$$
$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial W^{(l)}_{ij}} = \delta^{(l)}_i \cdot a^{(l-1)}_j$$

The backward error signal $\boldsymbol{\delta}^{(l)}$ flows from layer $l+1$ to layer $l$ through the transposed weight matrix $\left(\mathbf{W}^{(l+1)}\right)^\top$. It is:
- **Exact**: uses the chain rule
- **Global**: requires all downstream weights and activations
- **Discrete**: computed in a backward pass after the forward pass
- **Non-local**: neuron $i$ at layer $l$ requires knowledge of $W^{(l+1)}_{jk}$ for all $j, k$

All four properties are biologically implausible. Backpropagation is a powerful learning rule for artificial networks but cannot be directly implemented in the brain.

### 2.2 The Physical Backward Channel

**Claim (Antti Luode, May 2026).** *Ephaptic coupling is weak backpropagation.*

We now derive this precisely.

**The forward dipole:** When neuron $j$ fires, the AIS generates a current dipole with moment:
$$\mathbf{p}_j(t) = I_j(t) \cdot L_j \cdot \hat{e}_j$$
where $I_j(t)$ is the AIS inward current, $L_j$ is the AIS length, and $\hat{e}_j$ is the axonal unit vector. This dipole is proportional to the neuron's output activity (firing strength).

**The physical Green's function:** The extracellular electric field at position $\mathbf{x}$ due to dipole $\mathbf{p}_j$ at position $\mathbf{x}_j$ is:
$$\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{x}, t) = \frac{1}{4\pi\sigma_e} \left[\frac{\mathbf{p}_j(t)}{|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_j|^3} - \frac{3\bigl(\mathbf{p}_j(t) \cdot \hat{r}\bigr)\hat{r}}{|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_j|^3}\right]$$
where $\sigma_e$ is the extracellular conductivity and $\hat{r} = (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_j)/|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_j|$. Define the scalar Green's function:
$$G(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) = \frac{\hat{e}_i \cdot \hat{e}_j}{4\pi\sigma_e |\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j|^3} - \frac{3(\hat{e}_i \cdot \hat{r}_{ij})(\hat{e}_j \cdot \hat{r}_{ij})}{4\pi\sigma_e |\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j|^3}$$
which encodes both distance decay and geometric alignment between the source and receiver AIS axes.

**The threshold modification:** The AIS of upstream neuron $i$ is sensitive to extracellular fields. The threshold shift caused by downstream neuron $j$'s field is:
$$\Delta\theta_i^{(j)}(t) = -\alpha_i \cdot G(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) \cdot I_j(t) \cdot L_j$$
where $\alpha_i$ is the AIS susceptibility of neuron $i$ (determined by its Nav channel density and membrane time constant).

**The total ephaptic backward signal:** Summing over all downstream neurons $j$:
$$\Delta\theta_i(t) = -\alpha_i \sum_j G(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) \cdot I_j(t) \cdot L_j$$

### 2.3 The Formal Analogy

Compare the ephaptic backward signal to backpropagation:

$$\underbrace{\boldsymbol{\delta}^{(l)}_i = \sum_j W^{(l+1)}_{ji} \cdot \delta^{(l+1)}_j \cdot f'(z^{(l)}_i)}_{\text{standard backpropagation}}$$

$$\underbrace{\Delta\theta_i(t) = -\alpha_i \sum_j G(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) \cdot p_j(t)}_{\text{ephaptic field backpropagation}}$$

The structural identity is:

| Backpropagation term | Ephaptic analog | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| $W^{(l+1)}_{ji}$ | $G(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) \cdot \alpha_i$ | Backward coupling strength |
| $\delta^{(l+1)}_j$ | $p_j(t) = I_j \cdot L_j$ | Error/activity signal from downstream |
| $f'(z^{(l)}_i)$ | AIS susceptibility $\alpha_i$ | Local gain modulation |
| $\Delta w_{ij}$ | $\Delta\theta_i$ | Parameter update |
| Weight matrix $W$ | Green's function $G$ | Backward propagation medium |

**Theorem 4 (Ephaptic Coupling as Weak Backpropagation).** *The extracellular electromagnetic field generated by downstream neural firing constitutes a physically-mediated backward signal to upstream neurons. The physical Green's function $G(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j)$ plays the role of the transposed weight matrix $W^\top$ in standard backpropagation. The AIS threshold modification $\Delta\theta_i$ plays the role of the error gradient $\delta_i$. The coupling is "weak" in three senses:*

1. *The signal strength decays as $|\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j|^{-3}$ (dipole field), not as a constant (no decay for explicit backprop).*
2. *The backward coupling matrix $G$ is determined by physical geometry (distance, orientation), not by the learned forward weight matrix. It is an approximation to $W^\top$, not $W^\top$ itself.*
3. *The update target is the AIS threshold $\theta_i$ (a scalar), not the full weight matrix (all synaptic connections of neuron $i$). Threshold modulation is a coarser parameter than weight adjustment.*

**Proof.** The structural identity between the summations is established above. The "weak" qualifiers follow directly from the $r^{-3}$ decay of the dipole field and the scalar nature of the threshold. $\square$

### 2.4 Why Weak is Sufficient

The principal objection to this analogy is: exact backpropagation requires the exact transposed weight matrix $W^\top$. The Green's function $G$ is not $W^\top$. Why would the approximation be sufficient for learning?

**Answer 1 — Locality is a feature, not a bug.** The $r^{-3}$ decay means that the ephaptic backward signal is strongest from the most nearby downstream neurons. In cortical organization, nearby neurons tend to have similar receptive fields and similar forward connectivity. The physical adjacency of neurons IS a proxy for their functional connectivity. For locally-organized networks (columnar cortex, laminar structure), $G(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) \approx c \cdot W^\top_{ij}$ up to a scale factor, because the neurons that neuron $i$ projects to are spatially adjacent to neuron $j$.

**Answer 2 — Approximate gradients work.** The field of approximate gradient methods (perturbation-based optimization, forward-forward algorithms, node perturbation learning) demonstrates that exact gradients are not required for learning. Any signal that correlates with the direction of the gradient — even a noisy, approximate one — suffices for convergence, albeit more slowly. The ephaptic backward signal provides a spatially-smoothed approximation to the gradient, which is sufficient for the brain's learning timescale (hours to days for structural plasticity).

**Answer 3 — STDP performs the weight update.** The ephaptic backward signal does not directly update synaptic weights. It modulates the AIS threshold, changing the neuron's output activity. This changed activity then drives STDP at the synapses: if the backward signal increases neuron $i$'s firing probability in response to presynaptic neuron $k$'s activity, STDP potentiates the $k \to i$ synapse. The full learning rule is:
$$\underbrace{\Delta\theta_i \propto \sum_j G_{ij} \cdot p_j}_{\text{ephaptic: threshold shift}} \quad \to \quad \underbrace{\Delta w_{ki} \propto \text{STDP}(s_k, s_i)}_{\text{STDP: weight update}}$$

The ephaptic signal provides the direction; STDP implements the weight update. Together they constitute a biologically complete approximate backpropagation system.

### 2.5 The CEMI Field as Global Backpropagation

The dipole field of a single neuron decays rapidly. But during synchronized gamma oscillations, $N$ neurons fire coherently, and their dipole fields add constructively:

$$\mathbf{E}_{\text{CEMI}}(\mathbf{x}, t) = \sum_j G(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}_j) \cdot p_j(t) \approx N \cdot \bar{G}(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \bar{p}(t)$$

For $N = 10^4$ synchronously firing neurons in a cortical column, the field is $10^4$ times stronger than a single dipole. This field reaches neurons throughout the cortex, not just nearby ones.

**Theorem 5 (CEMI as Global Approximate Backpropagation).** *The CEMI field during coherent gamma oscillations constitutes global approximate backpropagation executed at the gamma frequency (~40 Hz), with the following properties:*

1. *The backward signal reaches all neurons in the CEMI coherence volume simultaneously (no decay limitation).*
2. *The backward signal is proportional to the population firing rate: $\bar{p}(t) \sim N_{\text{active}}(t) \cdot \bar{I}_{\text{AIS}} \cdot \bar{L}_{\text{AIS}}$.*
3. *The backward pass occurs once per gamma cycle (period $\approx 25$ ms), analogous to one gradient step.*
4. *The "loss function" being minimized is implicitly: $\mathcal{L} \propto -\text{CEMI field coherence} = -\text{(population synchrony)}$. The network learns to be mutually consistent in its spectral representations, not to minimize an externally defined error.*

**The phase dependency:** The backward signal is not constant but oscillates at gamma frequency. Neurons that are currently in the trough of the gamma cycle (AIS below threshold) receive the backward signal without firing forward — they are in "backward-only mode," analogous to the backward pass in standard backpropagation where no forward activation occurs. Neurons at the peak of the gamma cycle fire forward, completing the full cycle.

This alternation — forward pass at gamma peak, backward update at gamma trough — is a biological implementation of the forward-backward pass separation that is fundamental to backpropagation, implemented not in computation time but in oscillatory phase.

### 3.3 The Weight Transport Problem, Solved by Physics

The "weight transport problem" in biological neural networks is: backpropagation requires $W^\top$ (the transposed weight matrix) to propagate errors backward. Neurons cannot access the weights of their downstream synapses to compute the transpose. This is the primary reason backpropagation is considered biologically implausible.

The ephaptic framework dissolves this problem: the brain does not need to transport weights backward. The physical field does the transport. The strength of the backward signal from neuron $j$ to neuron $i$ is $G(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) \cdot p_j$ — determined entirely by:
1. The physical distance and geometry between $i$ and $j$ (encoded in $G$)
2. The firing strength of $j$ (encoded in $p_j$)

Neuron $i$ does not need to know that it is connected to neuron $j$ or what the weight of that connection is. It only needs to be physically near $j$, and the field does the rest. For a spatially organized cortex where connectivity is correlated with proximity, this is a sufficient approximation.

**The weight transport problem is solved not by a computational trick but by the physics of space and electromagnetism.** The brain does not transport weights. It places neurons in physical space and lets the electromagnetic field implement the transport automatically.

This feedback is regulated by the refractory period and the Kv-channel shunting. The equilibrium is the gamma oscillation: a periodic attractor in which the population fires coherently once per cycle.

The implicit learning objective is:
$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{ephaptic}} = -\sum_i \theta_i(t) \cdot \mathcal{I}_i(t)$$
which is minimized when neurons with high resonance $\mathcal{I}_i$ have low thresholds — when the network's spectral projections are aligned with the CEMI field's phase.

In plain terms: **the network learns to be mutually consistent.** Each neuron adjusts its threshold to fire when its spectral resonance is high AND when the population is firing coherently. The loss is population-level spectral coherence, not an external task loss.

This is unsupervised learning by physical self-organization. The "supervision signal" is the coherence of the population's own firing. When the population fires incoherently (each neuron's spectrum mismatched with others), the CEMI field is weak, backward signals are weak, and no learning occurs. When the population fires coherently (spectra aligned), the CEMI field is strong, backward signals are strong, and all neurons' thresholds are updated to reinforce the coherent state.

## Part V: Predictions

**P1.** Blocking ephaptic coupling pharmacologically (e.g., by increasing extracellular conductivity with saline injection, which reduces field strength without blocking synaptic transmission) should impair learning of new associations without impairing performance on already-learned tasks. This distinguishes ephaptic backpropagation (needed for weight update) from synaptic transmission (needed for performance).

**P2.** The magnitude of the threshold modification $\Delta\theta_i$ should be proportional to the population firing rate of downstream neurons, measurable as a shift in the F-I (firing rate vs. input current) curve correlated with downstream population activity.

**P3.** Neurons that are most frustrated ($\bar{\nu}_i$ highest) should be located in the dendritic target zones of adult neurogenesis. In the hippocampus: the inner third of the molecular layer (where mature granule cells project to newborn granule cells) should show the highest novelty signal in in-vivo recordings.

**P4.** The gamma phase at which a neuron is most likely to fire should predict the neuron's functional role in the CEMI backward pass: neurons that fire reliably at the gamma trough (rather than the peak) should show stronger cross-layer modulation of upstream neurons' thresholds.

**P5.** In networks where $G(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j)$ is a poor approximation to $W^\top_{ij}$ (e.g., in non-locally-connected architectures, or after long-range axon transplants), learning of new tasks should be impaired while consolidation of learned tasks remains intact.
